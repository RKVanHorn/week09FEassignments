Birds of the Mojave

Birds in the Mojave utilize many different strategies to survive in the desert. Some, such as the Costa's hummingbird are migratory visitors, spending time in the desert when there are abundant Spring flowers and rains. Others like roadrunners are year round residents who are skilled at catching lizards, rodents and insects depending on the time of the year. Some birds, like hummingbirds can go into a state called estivation where they lower their metabolic rate to conserve water and food resources. This state can be triggered by both high temperatures and low temperatures. Click on the dropdown tabs for each bird below to see how they find the three survival essentials: water, food and shelter.

Costa's Hummingbird

Calypte costae
Water Hummingbirds get all of their water from the nectar that they drink when foarging. They will also readily drink sugar water from backyard bird feeders.
Food Costa's hummingbirds eat nectar from desert plants as well as small insects. The nectar is a quick source of sugar and energy that enables them to fly quickly to catch small insects like gnats that provide the protein they need to survive.
Shelter Female hummingbirds will build nests for breeding, but otherwise, hummingbirds roost in trees usually hidden easily by the tree's foliage.

Greater Roadrunner

Geococcyx californianus
Water Roadrunners will drink water if they find it, but get the majority of the water they need from the food that they eat.
Food Roadrunners are omnivores. They are not very picky and will eat whatever they can find including small mammals, birds, reptiles, insects, fruits, seeds and other vegetation.
Shelter Roadrunners will roost in shrubby bushes overnight, but like hummingbirds, really only build a nest during breeding season.

Burrowing Owl

Athene cunicularia
Water Burrowing owls can get all of the water they need from the prey that they hunt, but will drink water if available.
Food Burrowing owls are ferocious predators and will hunt and catch anything small enough for them to attack safely. Their diet usually consists of insects, small lizards, rodents, snakes and other birds. They have even been known to hunt bats.
Shelter As their name suggests, burrowing owls live in burrows. Generally they will take over abandoned burrows. Burrowing owls that find a good burrow will stay with it for years raising a new clutch of owlets each year in the same burrow.

Gambel's Quail

Callipepla gambelii
Water Gambel's quail will drink water that is available, but can get enough to survive from the insects and cactus fruits that they like to eat.
Food Gambel's quail are mostly herbivores, with young birds eating the occasional insect. They usual diet consists of cactus fruits, berries, fresh plant shoots, seeds and flower buds.
Shelter Gambel's quail will roost in bushes and shrubs in a group called a covey. During breeding season, female quail nest on the ground. All their eggs hatch on the same day and from day 1 the chicks are ready to follow their parents. Seeing a family of quail running around is a common occurrence in summer!
