Reptiles of the Mojave

Reptiles truly evolved to live in harsh environments like the desert. Being ectothermic (cold-blooded) means that they can take advantage of the high temperatures and abundant sunshine of Mojave summers to support their metabolism and aid in things like digestion, waste processing and reproduction. Then what happens to reptiles in the winter when temperatures reach down near freezing? Reptiles go into a state of brumation, similar to the mammalian hibernation we see in bears in the winter. During brumation, their heart rates slow dramatically, digestive processes slow and they are able to survive the winter by "sleeping" it away. The desert tortoise is an animal most of us in Las Vegas are familiar with and we wait for Mojave Max to emerge from brumation each year, much like folks to the East wait for Punxsutawney Phil. Desert Tortoise

Gopherus agassizii
Water Desert tortoises have an uncanny abiity to sense when water is near and also use changes in barometric pressure to help them know when it is about to rain. They will wait by shallow depressions in the hard desert surface and drink the water that pools up in them. If there are no depressions, they have been known to dig their own! Deset tortoises bladders work just like a canteen, storing water for months at a time. During the rainy seasons in the desert, these amazing animals will drink as much as possible and then are able to store it for dry times. They are the ultimate water conservers!
Food Desert tortoises are herbivores, they eat things like grasses, forbes, cactus fruits and even cactus pads! The nice thing about being able to eat cactus is that cactus store a lot of water, which benefits the animal consuming them. Desert tortoise often spend their mornings and evenings wandering their home territory foraging for food. During the hot part of the day they will spend time in their burrows.
Shelter Desert tortoises really are the penultimate desert survivors. Their burrows are one of the reasons why. Desert tortoises have incredibly powerful front legs with claws that allow them to dig long burrows down into the hard desert ground. These burrows can be 6-7 feet long and go down close to 4 feet underground. The benefits to such a well constructed burrow are that they protect them from predators and they also provide a temperature break of up to 20 degrees from the surface temperature. In the summer this means a nice cool burrow and in the winter it means a burrow that maintains the perfect temperature for brumation.

Chuckwalla

Sauromalus ater
Water Chuckwallas have wonderfully thick, tough skin that helps them maintain water in their body despite the high temperatures in the Mojave. They are opportunistic water drinkers, but are also able to get the majority of the water they need from their diet. Reptiles tend to be quite efficient in their waste processing and use less water processing liquid wastes, passing urates, a thick pasty uric acid, instead of urine.
Food Chuckwallas are mainly herbivores, eating flowers, fruits and greens they find during they day. They will also opportunistically eat insects that are relatively easy to catch.
Shelter Chuckwallas tend to live in and around rock piles or areas with rocky outcroppings. They thrive in the high heat and are often seen out basking on top of rocks in temperatures around 110°F. Those rock piles provide protection from predators and they also hold heat well, so in the evening when the sun goes down, chuckwalla will go to sleep in the rocks which stay warm for them until morning. Like all the other reptiles in the Mojave, chuckwalla brumate through winter. Chuckwalla will use the crevices in the rocks when a predator is chasing them. They can wedge their bodies in small spaces and then expand their abdomen full of air like a ballon, making them almost impossible to dislodge.

Horned Lizard

Phrynosoma platyrhinos
Water Horned lizards use the shape of their body and the channels that run between their scales to collect water! During desert rains, horned lizards will stand in the rain and flatter their body out to help collect water and funnel it toward their mouth. The grooves between their scales act like water channels helping to move the water in the right direction allowing them to take advantage of even the smallest amount of precipitation.
Food Horned lizards are myrmecophagous, which means they eat ants. Harvester ant colonies are very common in the Mojave and if you find an ant colony, chances are likely that there is a horned lizard somewhere nearby. Harvester ants are venomous and have a painful sting as well as powerful mandibles that can pack a wicked bite. Horned lizards can eat harvester ants because they incapacitate the ants with a glob of mucus before they swallow them whole!
Shelter Horned lizards don't build specific burrows or shelters, instead they take advantage of the sandy desert ground. They are able to quickly bury their entire body in sand using a shimmy type motion. Once buried they are virtually invisible and protected from the hot sun. They can help control their body temperature by choosing warmer or cooler places to bury themself.

Gopher snake

Urocyon cinereoargenteus
Water Gopher snakes are opportunistic water drinkers, but can get the vast majority of the water they need to survive in the prey that they catch and consume.
Food Gopher snakes are constrictors, which means that they grab their prey and squeeze them to suffocate and prevent blood flow. Their usual diet consists of small mammals like rabbits and gophers (hence their name!) as well as birds, other snake and eggs.
Shelter Gopher snakes brumate (reptile hibernation) in the winter in abandoned burrows. Often times many different species of snakes will brumate together.
